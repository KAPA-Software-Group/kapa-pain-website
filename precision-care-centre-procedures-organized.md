## 1. Clinic Positioning / Overall Message

### Precision Care Centre - Multidisciplinary Expertise

At Precision Care Centre, the core message is that the clinic uses a multidisciplinary team of specialists working together to provide comprehensive, patient-centered pain care.

The team includes:

- Interventional Radiologists: deliver highly precise, image-guided treatments for accurate diagnosis and targeted, minimally invasive therapies.
- Spine Surgeons: provide expert evaluation of complex spine conditions and help guide patients when surgical options are appropriate.
- Anesthesiology specialists: bring advanced expertise in pain management and procedural care, with a focus on comfort and safety.
- Orthopedic Surgeons: provide specialized knowledge in joint and musculoskeletal conditions, helping with diagnosis and access to both surgical and non-surgical treatment options.
- Emergency Physicians: bring broad clinical expertise and also provide infusion-based therapies for complex and refractory pain conditions.
- Nurse Practitioners: support ongoing care, patient education, continuity, and coordination of personalized treatment plans.

Overall clinic message:

- The clinic integrates different specialties into one coordinated care pathway.
- The approach goes from diagnosis to treatment in a seamless way.
- The focus is on precise, effective, individualized care.
- The clinic's goals are to improve function, reduce pain, and enhance quality of life.

## 2. Chronic Pain Assessment & Medical Management

This section is more about the clinic's overall chronic pain care model than one single procedure.

### Main Idea

Precision Care Centre offers a comprehensive, multidisciplinary approach to the assessment and management of chronic pain. The goal is to accurately identify the source of pain and create a personalized, evidence-based treatment plan.

### Comprehensive Assessment

Every patient may go through:

- Review of medical history and prior treatments
- Focused physical examination
- Review of imaging such as X-ray, MRI, and CT
- Functional assessment and review of how pain affects daily life

Why this matters:

- It helps identify the underlying cause of pain.
- It helps guide the most appropriate treatment plan.

### Medical Management

Medical management may include:

- Non-opioid medications such as anti-inflammatories and neuropathic agents
- Targeted pharmacologic therapy based on the type of pain
- Infusion-based treatments for complex or refractory pain
- Coordination with primary care and other specialists

Core treatment philosophy:

- Focus on safe, effective, sustainable pain control
- Minimize reliance on opioids whenever possible

### Integrated Care Approach

The clinic combines:

- Interventional procedures such as nerve blocks, RFA, and injections
- Rehabilitation and physiotherapy guidance
- Lifestyle and activity modification
- Ongoing follow-up and reassessment

### Main Goals

- Reduce pain
- Improve function and mobility
- Enhance quality of life

## 3. Advanced & Specialized Procedures

This is the "higher-level procedures we offer" section.

### General Framing

Precision Care Centre offers advanced, minimally invasive procedures for complex and chronic pain conditions when standard therapies are not enough. These procedures are performed using image guidance, including ultrasound or fluoroscopy/X-ray, to support precision, safety, and optimal outcomes.

### Procedures Listed

#### Pulsed Radiofrequency Ablation (Pulsed RFA)

- A non-destructive treatment
- Uses controlled electrical energy to modulate nerve function
- Reduces pain without permanently damaging the nerve
- Often used in sensitive areas or in situations where preserving nerve function is important

#### Conventional Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA)

- Uses targeted heat to temporarily disrupt pain-transmitting nerves
- Intended to provide longer-lasting pain relief
- Commonly used for conditions such as facet joint pain and chronic joint pain

#### Cryoablation (Cryoneurolysis)

- Uses extreme cold to temporarily deactivate pain nerves
- Can provide effective pain relief with minimal tissue damage
- Commonly used for peripheral nerve pain and post-surgical pain

#### Genicular Artery Embolization (GAE)

- A minimally invasive procedure for knee osteoarthritis
- Works by reducing abnormal inflammation in the joint lining
- Intended to relieve pain and improve function without surgery

#### Vertebroplasty & Kyphoplasty

- Image-guided procedures used to treat vertebral compression fractures
- Medical cement is injected into the fractured bone
- Intended to stabilize the fracture, reduce pain, and improve mobility
- Kyphoplasty may also restore some vertebral height

## 4. Nerve Blocks

This section is its own service overview, even though some nerve blocks also show up elsewhere in the document.

### General Overview

Precision Care Centre offers a wide range of image-guided nerve blocks to help diagnose and treat pain. These are minimally invasive procedures performed using ultrasound or fluoroscopy/X-ray to make delivery to the target nerve precise and safe.

Nerve blocks can be used in two ways:

- Diagnostic: to identify the exact source of pain
- Therapeutic: to reduce inflammation and interrupt pain signals

### Common Nerve Blocks Performed

#### Spinal Nerve Blocks (Medial Branch Blocks)

- Used to diagnose and treat pain coming from the facet joints of the spine
- Commonly relevant in chronic neck pain and low back pain

#### Genicular Nerve Blocks (Knee)

- Target nerves around the knee
- Used to assess and treat chronic knee pain
- Includes pain after knee replacement

#### Peripheral Nerve Blocks

- Includes treatments for nerves in the shoulder (suprascapular), hip, ankle, and upper extremity
- Used to help manage joint-related and nerve-related pain

#### Selective Nerve Root Blocks

- Target specific spinal nerve roots
- Used to diagnose and treat radiating arm or leg pain
- Includes radiculopathy and sciatica

### Benefits of Nerve Blocks

- Minimally invasive with quick recovery
- Performed with image guidance for accuracy and safety
- Helps accurately identify the source of pain
- Can provide significant pain relief
- Can guide further treatment, including radiofrequency ablation

### Clinic Approach

The clinic uses nerve blocks as part of a structured, evidence-based treatment pathway. The main idea is to combine precise diagnosis with targeted therapy so that each patient gets care that is personalized and effective.

## 5. Nerve Hydrodissection

### What It Is

Nerve hydrodissection is described as a simple, minimally invasive procedure used to treat nerve pain.

How it works:

- A small needle is used
- A safe fluid is injected around the nerve
- The fluid used is saline and dextrose
- Ultrasound guidance is used for accuracy

What the fluid is meant to do:

- Gently separate the nerve from surrounding tissue
- Reduce pressure on the nerve
- Release scar tissue
- Decrease pain and irritation

### Types of Nerve Hydrodissection Treatments

#### Sciatic Nerve (Leg Pain / Sciatica)

- Used for pain that travels from the lower back or buttock down the leg
- Helps relieve nerve irritation causing sciatica

#### Median Nerve (Carpal Tunnel - Wrist/Hand)

- Used for carpal tunnel syndrome
- Helps with numbness, tingling, or weakness in the hand and fingers
- Can be an option for patients who want to avoid or delay surgery

#### Ulnar Nerve (Elbow / Hand)

- Used for numbness or tingling in the ring and little fingers
- Helps with pain along the inner forearm or hand
- Commonly used for cubital tunnel syndrome

#### Abdominal Wall Nerves

- Used for localized abdominal wall pain
- Useful for pain after surgery, injury, or strain

### What to Expect After the Procedure

Normal symptoms may include:

- Mild soreness or bruising at the injection site
- A feeling of fullness where the fluid was injected
- Temporary numbness or tingling

Pain relief expectations:

- Some patients feel better right away
- Others improve gradually over several days to weeks

### Aftercare

- Apply an ice pack for 10 to 15 minutes at a time
- Take acetaminophen (Tylenol) if needed
- Avoid heavy activity for 24 to 48 hours

### When to Contact the Clinic

Patients should contact the clinic if they experience:

- Increasing redness, swelling, or warmth
- Fever or chills
- Severe or worsening pain

### Why This Procedure

- Minimally invasive and does not involve surgery
- Quick recovery
- Performed with ultrasound for accuracy
- Can reduce pain and improve function

## 6. Spine & Joint Injections

This section contains several related procedures and should probably be treated as a grouped website section with subpages or expandable content blocks.

### 6.1 Diagnostic Injections

Examples listed:

- MBB (Medial Branch Block)
- Genicular Nerve Block (GNB)
- Other diagnostic blocks

#### Purpose

Diagnostic injections are used to determine the source of pain.

Specific examples:

- MBB (Medial Branch Block): identifies facet joint pain in the spine
- Genicular Nerve Block (GNB): identifies nerve-related knee pain
- Other diagnostic blocks: used for specific nerve-related pain conditions

Important diagnostic point:

- If the patient experiences short-term pain relief, that helps confirm the diagnosis

#### What to Expect

- Temporary pain relief lasting a few hours to up to 1 day
- Numbness in the affected area
- Mild soreness at the injection site
- Pain will return once the numbing medication wears off

#### Important Patient Role

The document strongly emphasizes that the patient's response is critical for diagnosis.

Patients are expected to:

- Track their pain carefully using the provided pain journal
- Bring the journal to the follow-up appointment

#### Activity Instructions

- Stay active on the day of the procedure
- Perform activities that normally cause the pain

Reason for that instruction:

- It helps determine whether the injection worked

### 6.2 Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA)

#### Purpose

RFA is used to provide longer-term pain relief.

How it works:

- It uses heat to temporarily disrupt the nerves that carry pain signals

Commonly used for:

- Facet joint pain
- Chronic knee pain, including pain after knee replacement
- Chronic joint pain when other treatments have failed

Expected duration:

- Relief may last several months, depending on nerve regrowth

#### What to Expect After RFA

- Mild soreness, burning, or swelling
- Temporary increase in pain in some patients
- Pain relief may take up to 4 weeks
- Full benefit is usually seen by 4 weeks

#### Aftercare

- Ice packs for 10 to 15 minutes at a time
- Take acetaminophen (Tylenol) if needed
- Light activity the next day
- Gradual return to normal activity over 3 to 5 days
- Avoid strenuous activity for 1 week

### 6.3 TFESI (Transforaminal Epidural Steroid Injection)

#### Purpose

TFESI is described as a targeted diagnostic and therapeutic injection used to reduce inflammation around spinal nerves.

Common conditions treated:

- Sciatica (leg pain)
- Cervical radiculopathy (arm pain)
- Herniated disc-related nerve pain
- Spinal stenosis

#### What to Expect

- Mild soreness at the injection site
- Temporary numbness or weakness due to local anesthetic
- Do not drive on the day of the procedure
- Temporary increase in pain may occur, sometimes described as a steroid flare
- Pain relief typically begins in 2 to 7 days
- In some cases, relief may take up to 2 weeks

### 6.4 Selective Nerve Root Block (SNRB)

#### Purpose

SNRB is used for both diagnosis and treatment of nerve pain.

Reasons it is used:

- Helps identify the exact nerve causing symptoms
- Often used when multiple nerves may be involved
- Commonly requested by spine surgeons

Diagnostic meaning:

- Short-term relief helps confirm the source of pain

#### What to Expect

- Temporary numbness, weakness, or heaviness in the limb
- Do not drive on the day of the procedure
- Immediate but short-term relief may occur
- Pain may return before the steroid effect begins
- Improvement typically occurs within 2 to 5 days
- In some cases, improvement may take up to 2 weeks

#### Important Patient Role

- Track pain using the provided journal
- Bring the journal to the follow-up appointment with the physician or surgeon

### 6.5 Aftercare for TFESI & SNRB

- Ice packs for 10 to 15 minutes at a time
- Take acetaminophen (Tylenol) if needed
- Avoid NSAIDs unless otherwise instructed
- Light activity the next day
- Gradual return to normal activity over 3 to 5 days

### 6.6 General Risks for All Procedures in This Section

These procedures are generally considered safe, but risks may include:

- Pain, bruising, or swelling
- Temporary numbness or weakness
- Infection (rare)
- Bleeding (rare)
- Nerve injury (very rare)
- Allergic reaction (rare)
- Incomplete or temporary relief

### 6.7 When to Contact the Clinic

Seek medical attention if there is:

- Increasing redness, swelling, or warmth
- Fever or chills
- Severe or worsening pain
- New or worsening weakness

## 7. Epidural Injections

This section overlaps with TFESI above, but it adds extra detail about the broader epidural category, imaging, medication components, and anticoagulant instructions.

### What Epidural Injections Are

Epidural injections are used to treat pain caused by irritated or inflamed spinal nerves.

How they are performed:

- Medication is placed into the epidural space around the spine
- Image guidance is used for accurate and safe needle placement
- The imaging method listed here is fluoroscopy/X-ray

### Types of Epidural Injections

#### Interlaminar Epidural

- Injection is given from the middle of the back or neck
- Medication spreads more broadly around multiple nerves
- Performed using fluoroscopy/image guidance

#### Transforaminal Epidural Steroid Injection (TFESI)

- Targets a specific nerve root more precisely
- Often used for pain that radiates into the arm or leg
- Performed using fluoroscopy/image guidance for precision

#### Caudal Epidural

- Injection is given near the tailbone, in the sacral area
- Provides broader coverage of lower spinal nerves
- Often used when prior surgery has caused scar tissue
- Performed using fluoroscopy/image guidance

### What Is Injected

The injection may include:

- Steroid: reduces inflammation
- Local anesthetic: provides short-term pain relief
- Contrast dye: confirms accurate needle placement under imaging

Important note:

- Patients should inform staff if they have any allergies to contrast dye

### Conditions Commonly Treated

- Sciatica, meaning leg pain from nerve irritation
- Cervical radiculopathy, meaning arm pain
- Herniated discs
- Spinal stenosis
- Persistent pain after spine surgery

### Blood Thinner (Anticoagulant) Instructions

If a patient is taking blood thinners such as Apixaban, Rivaroxaban, Heparin, or Warfarin:

- Cervical (neck) injections: stop 5 days before
- Lumbar interlaminar injections: stop 2 days before
- Patients should still follow their physician's specific instructions before stopping any medication

### What to Expect After the Procedure

#### Common Effects

- Mild soreness at the injection site
- Temporary numbness or weakness in the arms or legs
- Temporary tingling, especially with caudal injections

Important safety note:

- Do not drive on the day of the procedure if numbness or weakness is present

#### Pain Relief Timeline

- Pain may temporarily increase after the injection due to a steroid flare
- Relief typically begins in 2 to 7 days
- Full effect may take up to 2 weeks

#### Other Possible Effects

- Temporary increase in blood sugar
- Temporary increase in blood pressure
- Headache
- Dizziness
- Nausea

### Rare but Serious Risks

Patients should seek immediate medical attention for:

- Severe headache that gets worse when sitting or standing
- Significant or worsening weakness
- Loss of bowel or bladder control
- Severe or increasing pain

### Aftercare Instructions

- Apply ice packs for 10 to 15 minutes at a time
- Take acetaminophen (Tylenol) if needed
- Avoid strenuous activity for 24 to 48 hours
- Gradually return to normal activity over a few days

### Benefits of the Procedure

- Minimally invasive
- Performed with image guidance for safety and accuracy
- Can reduce inflammation and relieve pain
- May improve function
- May delay or avoid surgery

### Alternatives

- Medications
- Physiotherapy
- Activity modification
- Other injections
- Surgery in selected cases
- No treatment

## 8. Stellate Ganglion Block (SGB)

This section is more detailed and reads like a patient education handout.

### Introduction

The Stellate Ganglion Block (SGB) is described as a medical procedure that may provide relief from symptoms of:

- Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
- Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)
- Hyperarousal
- Anxiety

Important positioning note:

- The document says high-level evidence has documented efficacy
- It also says this is considered an off-label treatment for this condition

### Procedure Overview

- The SGB involves injection of a local anesthetic into the stellate ganglion, which is a nerve cluster in the neck
- The procedure is typically performed on the right side first
- A subsequent left-sided injection may be considered if necessary
- Ultrasound guidance is used for precision
- Procedural sedation may be involved

### Purpose of the Procedure

The goal is to reduce symptoms by temporarily blocking sympathetic nerve activity.

Expected result of that nerve block:

- Reduction in hyperarousal
- Reduction in anxiety
- Better ability to engage with other treatments such as psychotherapy and medication

### Expected Benefits

- Reduction in PTSD symptoms
- Reduction in CRPS
- Rapid onset of symptom relief
- Improved outcomes when combined with other treatments like psychotherapy

### Evidence Supporting SGB

- Clinical trials, including Olmsted et al., 2019, show significant improvement in PTSD symptoms following SGB
- Studies report positive outcomes in over 80% of treated patients
- Behavioral health providers recommend SGB as a beneficial tool for trauma-related disorders

### Potential Risks and Adverse Events

The document says the procedure is generally well tolerated, but the following can occur.

Temporary effects:

- Horner's Syndrome, including droopy eyelid, constricted pupil, and red eye
- Hoarseness
- Sensation of a lump in the throat
- Pain or redness at the injection site
- Temporary difficulty swallowing
- Temporary difficulty speaking

Rare side effects:

- Breathing changes, including shortness of breath
- Neck pain
- Hematoma formation
- Seizures due to inadvertent intravascular uptake of anesthetic
- Pneumothorax, meaning collapsed lung

### Post-Procedural Care Instructions

1. Observation: patients are monitored for 30 minutes after the procedure.
2. Restrictions: avoid driving for 24 hours and have a support person present.
3. Care: use ice on the injection site if needed and avoid warm drinks until normal sensation returns.
4. Diet: resume eating and drinking when the voice returns to normal.
5. Emergency: seek immediate medical attention for severe neck pain, difficulty breathing, or persistent side effects.

### Contraindications

- Allergy to local anesthetics such as bupivacaine / Sensorcaine

### Alternatives to SGB

- Psychotherapy
- Pharmacological treatments such as SSRIs
- Other PTSD interventions

## 9. Arthritis Joint Injections

### What They Are

Arthritis injections are used to relieve joint pain and inflammation.

How they are performed:

- Medication is injected directly into the joint
- Image guidance is used for accuracy and safety
- Imaging may be ultrasound or fluoroscopy/X-ray

### Types of Arthritis Injections

#### Corticosteroid Injection

- Reduces inflammation within the joint
- Provides pain relief
- Improves mobility
- Described as the most commonly used injection

#### Viscosupplementation (Hyaluronic Acid)

- Helps lubricate and cushion the joint
- May improve movement
- May reduce stiffness
- Commonly used for knee osteoarthritis
- The note says: ask your doctor to discuss further

#### Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) (if applicable at your clinic)

- Uses the patient's own blood to promote healing
- May improve pain and joint function over time
- The note says: ask your doctor to discuss further

### Common Joints Treated

- Knee
- Shoulder
- Hip
- Other small or large joints

### Conditions Treated

- Osteoarthritis
- Degenerative joint disease
- Inflammatory joint pain
- Post-traumatic joint pain

### What Is Injected

Depending on the treatment, injections may include:

- Corticosteroid: reduces inflammation
- Local anesthetic: provides short-term pain relief and can also be used as a diagnostic tool
- Hyaluronic acid: improves joint lubrication

### What to Expect After the Procedure

#### Common Effects

- Mild soreness or swelling at the injection site
- Temporary increase in pain, sometimes described as steroid flare
- Temporary numbness from the local anesthetic

#### Pain Relief Timeline

- Some patients feel relief within a few days
- Steroid injections typically work within 2 to 7 days
- Full benefit may take up to 2 weeks

### Aftercare Instructions

- Apply ice packs for 10 to 15 minutes at a time
- Take acetaminophen (Tylenol) if needed
- Avoid strenuous activity for 24 to 48 hours
- Gradually return to normal activities

### Benefits of the Procedure

- Minimally invasive
- Performed with image guidance for accuracy and safety
- Can reduce pain and improve joint function
- May delay or avoid surgery

### Risks and Possible Complications

Although generally safe, risks may include:

- Pain
- Swelling
- Bruising
- Infection (rare)
- Bleeding (rare)
- Allergic reaction (rare)
- Temporary increase in blood sugar, especially in diabetic patients
- Skin changes or fat thinning at the injection site (rare)
- Incomplete or temporary relief

### When to Contact the Clinic

Patients should seek medical attention for:

- Increasing redness, swelling, or warmth
- Fever or chills
- Severe or worsening pain
- Difficulty moving the joint

### Alternatives

- Medications
- Physiotherapy
- Activity modification
- Bracing or supports
- Surgery in selected cases
- No treatment
